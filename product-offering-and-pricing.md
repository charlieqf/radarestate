# Product Offering And Pricing

## Positioning

The current product should not be sold as a decision-grade development feasibility report.

It should be sold as a three-layer offer:

1. `Planning Radar`
2. `Validated Shortlist`
3. `Development Feasibility Memo`

The third layer is explicitly a separate product with separate pricing.

## 1. Planning Radar

### What it is

- Automated planning and activity watchlist
- Weekly change detection
- Precinct-level prioritisation
- First-pass risk adjustment

### What it is not

- Not parcel-level
- Not acquisition sign-off
- Not a final feasibility or valuation memo

### Typical output

- Hero Visual Pack
- Top 10 Insights
- Weekly Radar
- Dashboard / client portal

### Pricing hypothesis

- `AUD 15-20 / week`
- Or monthly equivalent for retained access

### Who it suits

- Small developers
- Buyer's agents
- Acquisition teams that want change detection and filtering

## 2. Validated Shortlist

### What it is

- Weekly or fortnightly radar plus
- stronger automated validation for top `3-5` precincts or sites
- Stronger freshness and QA standard
- Explicit delta summary

### Core value

- Not just “what moved”
- Also “what we checked” and “what still holds up after validation”

### Typical output

- Weekly delta summary
- Validated shortlist note
- Annotated risk evidence
- validation flags

### Pricing hypothesis

- `AUD 25-30 / week`
- Higher if tailored to named target precincts

### Who it suits

- Developers who already know their target corridor
- Buyers who care more about triage quality than total coverage

## 3. Development Feasibility Memo

### What it is

This is the “hard information” layer developers actually use when deciding whether a site deserves serious pursuit.

It should be sold separately from the automated radar.

### Required content

- zoning / permissible use
- FSR / height / controls
- lot size / frontage / site dimensions
- assembly feasibility
- ownership context
- nearby comps / market context
- indicative residual or pricing logic

### What makes it different

- Moves from precinct-level signal to site-level assessment
- Requires stricter QA and stronger structured data coverage
- Often needs licensed or harder-to-access data inputs

### Pricing structure

Recommended as a separate scoped product, not included in the weekly subscription.

Pricing hypothesis:

- `AUD 150-250` for a light screening memo
- `AUD 300-600+` for a fuller feasibility-style memo

The exact price should vary by:

- number of candidate sites
- whether ownership checks are included
- whether comps are from public or licensed sources
- whether residual pricing is indicative or more developed

### Why separate pricing matters

- Different workload profile
- Different reliability expectation
- Different buyer expectation
- Different data dependency profile

## Packaging Recommendation

### Base offer

- Sell `Planning Radar` first

### Upgrade path

- Upgrade to `Validated Shortlist`

### High-value add-on

- Sell `Development Feasibility Memo` separately, per site or per brief

## Practical Sales Framing

### Good framing

- “automated planning radar”
- “validated shortlist”
- “site feasibility memo”

### Bad framing

- “full acquisition decision report” for the current radar product
- “development feasibility” when the output is still precinct-level

## Short Version

- `Radar` sells time-saving and change detection
- `Validated shortlist` sells better triage
- `Feasibility memo` sells decision support

These should not be priced or marketed as the same thing.
